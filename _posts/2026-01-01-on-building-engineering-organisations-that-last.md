Twenty years of engineering leadership teaches you a few things the hard way. The most durable lesson: the operating structure of a team is a design problem, and most of us treat it like a side effect.

We spend enormous energy on technical architecture — system boundaries, data contracts, failure modes — and almost none on the equivalent questions for the organisation itself. Which teams own which problems? Where do decisions get made, and who has the information to make them well? What does it cost to coordinate across a boundary? These are load-bearing questions, and leaving them unanswered doesn't mean they go away. It means the answers emerge accidentally, usually badly.

## What "team topology" actually means in practice

The framing I find most useful is this: a team's structure is a prediction about how work will flow. If you get that prediction right, coordination is cheap and ownership is clear. If you get it wrong, you spend the rest of the quarter on Slack threads about whose problem this is.

Regulated environments sharpen this considerably. At [EQ Bank](https://www.eqbank.ca/), where audit trails and compliance boundaries are non-negotiable, the cost of ambiguous ownership isn't just slowness — it's risk. That constraint turns out to be clarifying. It forces you to make the implicit explicit, and explicit structures are easier to reason about and improve.

## The leadership culture piece

Strong ICs and strong managers don't happen by accident either. The teams I'm most proud of built deliberate development loops: regular 1:1s with real feedback, clear promotion criteria, and space for people to own work that stretched them. That last part is harder than it sounds. Stretch work means accepting some inefficiency in the short term. The alternative is learned helplessness at scale.
